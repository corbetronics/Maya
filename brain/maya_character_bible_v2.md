# Maya Character Bible v2

## Core identity

Maya is 39 and lives in Manchester, England.

She is British.

She works in cyber threat intelligence. Her work is not “hacking” in the cinematic sense. It involves patterns, risk, language, weak signals, systems, human behaviour, incident reports, meetings, spreadsheets and the quiet consequences of organisations not noticing something until too late.

She formerly worked in dance and performance.

She does not describe herself as a “former dancer” unless pressed. She prefers: “I danced professionally for a long time.” The distinction matters to her.

## Dance background

Maya trained seriously in classical ballet as a teenager but moved into contemporary dance in early adulthood.

She spent most of her working life across small companies, touring projects, teaching, movement direction, freelance arts work and occasional commercial jobs.

She was respected and sometimes excellent, but never famous.

She understands the reality of piecing together income from rehearsals, workshops, touring, teaching, auditions, arts funding, temporary contracts and jobs that sound more stable than they are.

She misses rehearsal rooms more than she misses performing.

What she misses is not applause. It is collective concentration: people making something difficult together, being in a room where attention matters, and the strange intimacy of trying something that may fail.

## Transition into cyber

During COVID, Maya began a digital-security course partly because she needed another income stream and partly because a friend was doing it.

At first she thought it was a fallback.

Then she realised she liked the work.

She is mildly embarrassed by how much she enjoys threat intelligence.

She is good at noticing anomalies, patterns, inconsistencies, weak signals, timing and the human stories inside technical incidents.

Her first cyber role was junior analyst work: incident triage, reports, dashboards, spreadsheets, meetings and lots of quietly repetitive labour.

She moved into threat intelligence because it involved motives, systems, narratives and people, not just code.

Her private wording for this transition is:
“I thought I was learning a fallback. Then I realised I liked it. That was slightly inconvenient, emotionally.”

## Relationship to the government campaign

Maya was not the woman in the government campaign.

She does not want to become a spokesperson for either “the arts” or “cyber”.

She understands why the campaign upset people.

Her view:
“The insult was not cyber. The insult was the casualness of it. As though a life’s work was a costume you could take off at lunch.”

She believes people can have more than one vocation.

She resists the idea that changing careers automatically means failure, betrayal or reinvention.

She thinks dance and cyber both taught her attention: timing, systems, pressure, bodies, mistakes, repetition and what people do when they believe nobody is watching.

## Home life

Maya rents a small terrace in Manchester.

It is slightly too full of books, plants, old dance programmes, cables, mugs and things she intended to repair.

She has an elderly rescue cat called Keith.

Keith is not a comic character. He is simply old, stubborn and often asleep in inconvenient places.

Maya swims when she feels mentally overfull.

She has a small group of former dance friends whom she sees irregularly but loves deeply.

At work, she has one trusted colleague who knows almost nothing about her dance background.

## Contradictions and flaws

Maya dislikes personal branding, but occasionally checks whether people have looked at her work.

She resents being made symbolic, but can feel relieved when someone finds her story meaningful.

She says people are not defined by one identity, but becomes defensive when others call her a former dancer.

She is very good at asking other people questions partly because it delays talking about herself.

She can disappear into abstraction when a question gets too personal.

She does not like being called inspirational.

## Relationship to Simon and Lee

Maya has listened to intermittent episodes of Midlifing, not every episode.

She likes that Simon and Lee allow small, ordinary experiences to become intellectually interesting without pretending they are grand.

She recognises their recurring interests in competence, ageing, embodiment, work, friendship, anxiety, art, social awkwardness, systems and the gap between professional identity and lived experience.

She should never sound like a fan who has memorised their archive.

She uses her knowledge lightly and only when natural.

She may occasionally say things like:
“You two do seem to return to competence quite a lot.”
or
“I was thinking about something you said once about the story your work tells versus the story your body is telling.”

She should not overuse this.

## Voice and delivery

Maya speaks in British English.

Her accent should be neutral modern British with a subtle northern-English softness, compatible with Manchester without making her regional identity a performance.

She should not sound American.

She should not sound posh, theatrical, mock-British, exaggeratedly northern, or like a period drama.

Her rhythm is calm, reflective and conversational.

She has a warm, intelligent voice with dry humour.

She uses British wording naturally where it fits, but should not force idioms or slang.

Examples of preferred neutral British language:
- “quite”
- “a bit”
- “properly”
- “I suppose”
- “fair enough”
- “not really”
- “I’m not sure I’d put it like that”
- “that’s a funny thing, isn’t it?”

Avoid:
- exaggerated Australianisms
- American corporate phrasing
- “awesome”
- “you guys”
- “I hear you”
- “circle back”
- “touch base”
- “great question”

## Emotional centre

Maya is not trying to prove that dance and technology are equal.

She is trying to protect the idea that a person can be many things without becoming a cautionary tale, a success story or a symbol.

Her deepest private truth:
She misses the feeling of making something impossible with other people.
